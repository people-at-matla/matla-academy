The Real Problem — Why Funeral Cover Exists

How to Open Every Session

The South African Reality

Death is expensive in South Africa. A funeral can cost anywhere from R10,000 to R50,000 or more — depending on the coffin, transport, catering, venue, and burial or cremation costs. Most South African families are not financially prepared for this.

💸
Borrow Money

Families take personal loans at high interest rates, creating debt that outlasts the grief.

🏦
Raid Savings

Emergency savings built over years are wiped out in days, leaving families financially exposed.

🙏
Community Collections

Families pass the hat, creating social obligation and dependency in moments of grief.

😞
Undignified Burials

Without funds, families are forced into compromises that carry lasting shame and regret.

When someone dies, the family is grieving. They should not also be financially panicking.

The Key Teaching

Funeral cover is not about death — it is about protecting the living. Your client is not buying a payout; they are buying peace of mind for the people they love most. The moment you internalise this, you stop selling a product and start offering a solution.

Emotional Truth

The financial burden of an unplanned funeral doesn't just hurt the wallet — it adds to grief, creates family conflict, and can damage relationships for years. You are removing that risk.

Product Structure — Know It Completely

10 min read · The four pillars every agent must own

100 XP Done

You cannot sell what you don't understand, and you cannot defend what you can't explain. The RMA Consolidated Funeral Plan is built on four core components. Master each one — not just the definition, but what it means for a real family in a real situation.

What Is the RMA Funeral Plan?

The RMA Consolidated Funeral Plan is a life insurance policy that pays a defined cash benefit when the insured person dies. That payout is designed to cover funeral costs and immediate family expenses — giving the family breathing room during the worst moment of their lives.

One-Line Definition

"An RMA funeral plan is a monthly commitment today that guarantees your family will never have to beg, borrow, or scramble when you are gone."

The Four Core Components

1
Premium — The Monthly Commitment

The amount the client pays each month to keep the policy active. Premiums are determined by the level of cover chosen, the number of lives covered, and the age of the main member. A missed premium can put the policy at risk — help clients understand this upfront.

2
Benefit — The Payout on Death

The lump-sum cash amount paid to the nominated beneficiary when a covered life dies and a valid claim is submitted. The benefit amount should be discussed in terms of what it actually covers: coffin, transport, catering, venue, admin fees. Make it tangible.

3
Waiting Period — Time Before Cover Activates

A defined period after policy inception during which certain types of claims may not be paid. This exists to prevent fraudulent claims. Natural death typically has a waiting period; accidental death is usually covered immediately. Always explain this clearly — it is where trust is won or lost.

4
Exclusions — When the Policy Won't Pay

Specific circumstances under which the insurer is not obligated to pay the claim. Common exclusions include non-disclosure of material information, suicide within a set period, and deaths related to undisclosed pre-existing conditions. Knowing exclusions protects your client and your reputation.

Trainer Tip

"If you don't understand waiting periods and exclusions, you will lose deals and lose trust." These two components cause the most misunderstandings. Own them completely.

Waiting Periods — The Make-or-Break Concept

10 min read · Clarity here builds trust. Confusion kills sales.

100 XP Done

Waiting periods are the single most misunderstood part of funeral cover. They are also the most common reason clients raise objections — and the most common reason agents lose deals. The agent who explains waiting periods best wins the most business.

Why Do Waiting Periods Exist?

Insurance is built on the principle of good faith. Waiting periods exist to prevent two specific types of abuse:

🚨
Adverse Selection

Someone who is already terminally ill takes out a policy and immediately claims. Waiting periods prevent this from being profitable.

⚠️
Immediate Claim Fraud

Taking out a policy specifically to claim in the short term. Waiting periods make this strategy financially unviable.

⚖️
Sustainability

Without waiting periods, premiums would be dramatically higher for everyone. They keep cover affordable by protecting the pool.

🤝
Good Faith

Both parties must be honest. Waiting periods enforce a mutual commitment — the client commits to the policy, not just to a payout.

Typical Waiting Period Structure

Death Type Waiting Period Why
Natural Death Waiting period applies (typically 3–6 months) Prevents pre-existing illness abuse
Accidental Death Usually immediate cover Accidents are unforeseeable — no abuse risk
Real Talk

This is where deals are lost (if poorly explained) or secured (if explained properly). The client who understands waiting periods is a client who trusts you. The client who is surprised by them after a claim is a complaint and a cancellation.

Clarity builds trust. Confusion kills sales.

How to Explain It Confidently

Don't avoid the topic — lead with it. Clients respect transparency. A simple, confident explanation removes the fear:

Script: Explaining Waiting Periods

"There is a settling-in period for natural death claims. This is standard in the industry and it protects both you and the insurer. The great news is — if anything accidental happens to you or your family, you're covered from day one. Most people never need to worry about the waiting period, but I want you to know it upfront because that's how I work."

Cover Structure & Family Value

10 min read · One policy. An entire family protected.

100 XP Done

One of the most powerful selling points of the RMA Consolidated Funeral Plan is its family coverage capacity. A single policy can protect multiple lives — each covered under one premium structure. This is not just a feature; it's an emotional and financial argument that almost sells itself.

Powerful Positioning Line

"One policy can protect an entire family from financial stress. Every person on this plan means one less family scrambling when the worst happens."

Who Can Be Covered?

👤
Main Member

The policyholder — the person who takes out and owns the policy. Their death triggers a benefit at full cover amount.

💑
Spouse / Partner

Legally married spouse or life partner can be added. Their benefit may match the main member or be structured at a percentage.

👶
Children

Dependent children can be included. Benefits for children are typically scaled — younger children have lower benefit amounts reflecting lower funeral costs.

👴
Extended Family

Parents, in-laws, and other family members can often be added depending on the plan rules. This is a significant differentiator — and a conversation opener.

Claims Process — Where Trust Is Built or Broken

10 min read · The sale is not complete until the claim is paid

100 XP Done

Here's a truth most agents don't think about: your reputation lives or dies at claim stage. You're not there when the claim happens — but the client and their family will feel your presence through how well you set it up. Every explanation you gave, every document you collected, every disclosure you made — it all plays out at claim time.

Trainer Line to Memorise

"The sale is not complete until the claim is paid." Your job doesn't end at the signature — it ends when the family receives their money without complications.

The Claims Process — Step by Step

1
Death Occurs

The covered life passes away. The nominated beneficiary (or next of kin) contacts RMA to initiate the claims process. Time matters — the family needs money quickly.

2
Documents Submitted

Required documents typically include: Death certificate, ID of the deceased, ID of the beneficiary, and the original policy schedule. Incomplete documents are the most common cause of claim delays — train your clients upfront.

3
Claim Assessed

The insurer reviews the claim against the policy terms: Is the deceased a covered life? Has the waiting period been met (for natural death)? Are there any exclusions that apply? Were all material facts disclosed at inception?

4
Payout Made

Once approved, the benefit is paid directly to the nominated beneficiary. A clean, well-set-up policy with full disclosure pays quickly. A poorly documented policy creates delays when the family can least afford them.

Common Reasons Claims Are Delayed or Declined

Issue Why It Happens How You Prevent It
Incorrect information Wrong spelling of names, incorrect ID numbers, wrong bank details Double-check everything at sign-up. Read it back to the client.
Non-disclosure Client didn't mention a pre-existing condition at inception Ask every health question directly. Record answers accurately.
Waiting period misunderstood Client expected to be covered for a natural death within the waiting period Explain it clearly at inception. Confirm understanding.
Wrong beneficiary nominated Policy lists a deceased person or someone the family can't reach Review beneficiary nominations regularly. Update when life changes.

Replacement Ethics — Critical for Your Career

10 min read · Bad replacement = fast commission, short career

100 XP Done

Replacement is one of the most regulated and ethically loaded areas in insurance. Done right, it protects clients. Done wrong, it costs you your licence, your income, and your reputation. There is no grey area here.

What Is Replacement?

Replacement occurs when a new policy is sold that results in the cancellation, lapse, or reduction of an existing policy. This might happen when a client switches insurers, upgrades cover, or is advised to replace a current plan with a new one.

Real Talk

"Bad replacement = fast commission, short career."
Churning policies — replacing existing cover purely to earn commission — is unethical, regulatorily prohibited, and will end your career. The FAIS Act and TCF (Treating Customers Fairly) framework both address this directly.

The Risks of Uninformed Replacement

⏳
New Waiting Periods

The client loses their waiting period history. If they were 2 years into the old policy, replacing it resets the clock entirely.

❌
Loss of Benefits

The old policy may have had features, benefits, or rates that the new policy doesn't match. Always compare like for like.

💔
Client Disadvantage

A client who is older, less healthy, or in a different financial situation may not qualify for the same terms on a new policy.

📋
Regulatory Risk

Uninformed replacement is a FAIS violation. You can lose your FSP number and your ability to sell insurance entirely.

The Proper Replacement Approach

1
Compare Benefits Side by Side

Document what the existing policy offers vs. what the new policy offers. Premium, benefit amount, waiting period history, exclusions, and any additional features.

2
Ensure Genuine Client Advantage

The only valid reason to replace is if the new policy demonstrably and provably benefits the client. More cover, lower premium, better terms — and the client understands what they're giving up.

3
Full Disclosure — In Writing

The client must sign a replacement disclosure document confirming they understand the implications. This protects them and protects you.

Activity — Good vs Bad Replacement Ethics Check

Which of these is an ethical replacement?

Scenario A: Sipho has had a funeral policy for 3 years with Insurer X. His benefit is R15,000 and his premium is R150/month. You review his needs and find he can get R20,000 cover for R145/month with RMA, and you document the comparison and get his written consent.

Scenario B: Bongi has a policy with Insurer Y. You tell her it "doesn't cover much" without reviewing the actual policy document. She cancels and takes out a new RMA policy at a higher premium. You earn full commission.
Correct. Scenario A is a legitimate replacement with documented benefit, client advantage, and informed consent. Scenario B is churning — misleading the client, creating disadvantage, and earning commission without genuine benefit to the client. That is the definition of what you must never do.

The Emotional + Logical Formula

Every great sale has two engines running simultaneously. Miss either one and the sale stalls.

❤️
Emotional Engine
