What Happens Without a Will — Intestate Succession

If a person dies without a valid will (called dying intestate), the Intestate Succession Act determines who inherits. The law distributes the estate according to a fixed formula — regardless of what the deceased may have wanted. Common outcomes:

1
Assets Frozen Immediately

The Master of the High Court freezes the estate. The surviving spouse cannot access bank accounts, vehicles, or property until administration is complete — often 2–3 years.

2
State Appoints an Executor

Without a nominated executor in a will, the Master appoints one. This can be a stranger to the family, and the process is far slower than a nominated executor who knows the estate.

3
Children May Not Inherit from a Partner

Unmarried partners have no legal claim. Stepchildren inherit nothing without specific bequests. The law does not know your family — only a will does.

4
Minor Children's Inheritance Goes to the Guardian's Fund

Without a testamentary trust in the will, all assets destined for minor children are paid into the state-run Guardian's Fund — known for slow, bureaucratic access.

5
Massive Costs Consume the Estate

Executor fees, conveyancing fees, Master's fees, trustee fees, and VAT can collectively consume 10–25% of the gross estate value — often more for properties and complex assets.

The Brutal Truth

A family that loses the breadwinner AND has a frozen estate for 3 years while paying estate fees can lose everything they built. A will and an LPP policy change that outcome completely. This is what you're preventing.

The Last Will & Testament — The Foundation Document

10 min read · Master the document that protects everything else

100 XP Done

A Last Will and Testament is a legal document that records a person's final wishes for the distribution of their estate after death. Under South Africa's Wills Act 7 of 1953, a valid will must meet strict formal requirements — otherwise it may be declared invalid by the Master of the High Court.

The One-Line Definition

"A will is a letter to the law, telling it exactly what you want done with everything you built — signed while you can still sign it."

Legal Requirements for a Valid Will

✍️
Age 16+

The testator (person making the will) must be at least 16 years old.

🧠
Sound Mind

Must be of sound and disposing mind at the time of signing — not under duress, coercion, or incapacity.

📝
In Writing

Must be written (typed or handwritten). Verbal wills carry no legal weight in SA.

🖊️
Signed on Every Page

Every page must be signed by the testator in the presence of two competent witnesses.

👥
Two Witnesses

Both witnesses must sign the last page. Neither witness nor their spouse may be a beneficiary — or they forfeit their inheritance.

📅
Dated

The will must be dated. If multiple wills exist, the most recent valid will governs the estate.

The True Cost of Dying — Numbers That Shock

10 min read · Understand the fees that devour an unprotected estate

100 XP Done

Most people assume that when they die, their family gets everything. The truth is that before a single rand reaches a beneficiary, a series of legally mandated fees must be paid — often amounting to tens of thousands, sometimes hundreds of thousands of rands. These fees don't care about your circumstances. They are percentages of everything you built.

The Hard Truth — Use This In Every Presentation

On a R2 million estate (a modest home + savings), the fees before your family receives anything can reach R150,000 to R250,000+. These fees are not optional. They are the law.

The Three Fee Categories That Hit Every Estate

Executor Fees
3.5% + VAT
On the gross estate value. On a R2M estate = R79,800 before a cent reaches the family. Negotiable in theory — rarely in practice.
Trustee Fees
1.6% / year
On trust assets, charged annually for as long as the trust exists. A R1M trust = R16,000 per year — year after year until beneficiaries reach majority.
Conveyancing Fees
~R15,000+
To transfer a R500,000 property. Plus Master's fees and VAT. A transfer is not just a signature — it is a legal process with costs attached to every step.

The 30-Day Survival Clause — A Hidden Danger

Most wills contain a 30-day survival clause (also called a commorientes clause). This states that a beneficiary must survive the testator by at least 30 days to inherit. The intention is to avoid assets passing to someone who dies shortly after. But when both people are involved in the same accident, this clause creates dangerous gaps.

Real Scenario — Walk Every Client Through This

Thabo and his wife Nomsa are in a car accident. Thabo dies immediately. Nomsa dies 20 days later. Under the 30-day survival clause, Nomsa never inherited — so her heirs don't benefit from Thabo's estate. The assets bypass Nomsa completely and may go to unintended heirs. A good Capital Legacy will addresses this precisely.

Liquidity — The Most Ignored Problem

Even if the estate has high value — a house, a car, investments — those assets are frozen during the winding-up period. The family needs cash for living expenses while they wait. This is the liquidity crisis. Bills don't pause because someone died:

🏠
Mortgage Payments

Bond repayments don't stop. Miss them and the bank begins foreclosure proceedings — on the grieving family.

🎓
School Fees

Children's education doesn't wait. Without liquid funds, children may be pulled out of school mid-year.

🍽️
Daily Living Costs

Groceries, utilities, transport. Needs that continue regardless of what's happening in the Master's Office.

⚰️
Funeral Costs

Up to R50,000 or more — needed immediately, before any estate assets are released.

A wealthy estate on paper can leave a family financially stranded in reality. Liquidity is not a luxury — it is survival.

The Three Trust Types in South African Wills

Testamentary trusts (trusts created by a will) protect assets intended for beneficiaries who cannot manage them alone. There are three main trust types used in Capital Legacy wills:

Children's Trust
Most Common

Holds assets for minor beneficiaries until they reach a specified age (typically 18, 21, or 25). The trustee manages the funds for the child's education, maintenance, and welfare. Avoids the Guardian's Fund entirely.

Provider's Trust
Business Owners

Designed to provide for financially dependent beneficiaries (a disabled child, an elderly parent, a dependent sibling) over the long term. Assets are managed by trustees and distributed at their discretion based on needs.

Widow's Trust
Blended Families

Protects assets for a surviving spouse while also ensuring children from a prior relationship eventually inherit. The spouse benefits during their lifetime; the capital passes to the children on their death. Prevents estate depletion.

When to Recommend Which Trust

Minor children present? → Children's Trust. Long-term dependent? → Provider's Trust. Blended family or second marriage? → Widow's Trust. More than one situation may apply — multiple trusts in a single will are common and recommended.

Key Will Clauses — Expand Each to Learn

This prevents assets from flowing through two estates in quick succession, doubling estate costs. Always explain this clause to clients with spouses or elderly parents as beneficiaries.

"If something happens to both of you in the same accident, this clause ensures your children still inherit directly."

"Is there anything specific — a vehicle, a piece of jewellery, a property — that you want to go to a specific person?"

If a child is in a marriage in community of property and inherits R500,000, their spouse is legally entitled to half. This clause protects the inheritance for the intended beneficiary alone. "Do you want your daughter's inheritance to be protected if her marriage breaks down? This clause does exactly that."

Without a collation clause, all children inherit equally regardless of prior advances.
