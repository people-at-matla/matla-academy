01

The Mindset Shift — From Pushy to Purposeful

8 min read · The foundation everything else is built on

100 XP Done

The biggest mistake new advisors make has nothing to do with product knowledge, scripts, or closing techniques. It's this: they walk into every interaction trying to get a sale instead of solve a problem.

That single shift in intention changes everything — your tone, your questions, your listening, your energy. Clients feel it instantly. You can't fake genuine interest, and you can't hide self-interest either.

The Core Distinction

Transactional selling starts with the product and works backwards to the person. Consultative selling starts with the person and works towards the right solution — even if that solution is "not right now."

Transactional Advisor Consultative Advisor
Talks 70% of the time Listens 70% of the time
Leads with product features Leads with discovery questions
Rushes to the close Takes time to build trust
Treats every client the same Adapts to each person's situation
Measures success by daily sales Measures success by client outcomes
🎯
Credibility

They believe you know your product. Built through expertise and preparation.

🔄
Reliability

They believe you'll follow through. Built through consistency and callbacks.

❤️
Intimacy

They feel safe being honest with you. Built through empathy and listening.

⚠️
Self-Interest

The trust-killer. The moment clients sense you care more about commission than them — you've lost.

"People don't care how much you know until they know how much you care."

02

Understanding Your Client's World

8 min read · The art of asking the right questions

100 XP Done

You cannot solve a problem you don't understand. Yet most advisors spend the first five minutes of a call talking — about themselves, their company, their product. By the time they ask a question, the client has mentally checked out.

The best advisors operate on one rule: listen to understand, not to respond. When a client feels genuinely heard, their resistance drops, their honesty increases, and their trust in you grows naturally.

The 70/30 Rule

In a great consultative call, the client should speak 70% of the time. You speak 30% — mostly asking questions. If you're talking more than the client, you're pitching, not consulting.

The Discovery Question Framework

1
Background Questions

Understand their current situation. "Tell me a bit about your current financial setup — do you have any cover in place?"

2
Need Questions

Uncover what keeps them up at night. "What's your biggest financial concern right now — for yourself, or your family?"

3
Impact Questions

Make the risk real and personal. "If something happened to you tomorrow, how would your family cope financially for the first six months?"

4
Vision Questions

Let them describe the ideal outcome. "What would ideal financial security look like for your family — what would that feel like?"

Stated vs Unstated Needs

A client says: "I want cheap cover." That's their stated need. Their unstated need is usually: "I want to feel like I'm doing enough for my family." Sell to the unstated need and you'll close far more conversations.

"The advisor who asks the best questions wins — not the one with the best pitch."
Ask, Don't Tell Activity

Which response demonstrates real consultative listening?

A client says: "I'm not sure I need life cover — I'm young and healthy."
How should you respond?
Exactly right! Instead of arguing or agreeing, you ask questions that make the client think about their own reality. The impact questions do the selling for you — you're just holding the mirror.

03

The Conversation Framework

8 min read · Structure turns good conversations into great outcomes

100 XP Done

Without structure, even the most gifted advisors drift. They miss key steps, spend too long in the wrong phase, and lose the sale not because the client wasn't interested — but because the conversation lost momentum.

The S.T.A.R. Model is your framework. It's not a script — it's a navigation system. Internalize it and every conversation has direction.

📍
S — Situation

Where are they now? Understand their current financial reality before anything else.

⚡
T — Trigger

What event or fear prompted this conversation? Find the emotional driver behind the need.

🔍
A — Alternatives

What have they tried or considered? Understanding past attempts avoids retreading ground.

🎯
R — Resolution

Show how your solution bridges exactly the gap they've described. Mirror their language.

The Golden Time Flow

1
Build Rapport · 2–3 min

Name, a shared reference, acknowledge their time. Don't skip this — it sets the emotional tone.

2
Discovery Questions · 8–10 min

Use your 4-question framework. Let them talk. Take mental (or real) notes. Never interrupt.

3
Summarise What You Heard · 2 min

"So what I'm hearing is…" — This is the most powerful line in consulting. It proves you listened. It creates an instant connection.

4
Present Your Solution · 5–7 min

Link every feature back to what they told you. Don't present the whole product — only the parts that solve their specific problem.

5
Handle Objections + Close · 5–8 min

Welcome objections as questions. Address, confirm, move to natural close. Never rush, never beg.

The Mirror Technique

When presenting, use the client's exact words. If they said "I just want my kids to be okay" — your close is: "This plan ensures your kids are okay, no matter what happens." Not your words. Theirs.

Scenario: What's Missing? Activity
An advisor spends 3 minutes on rapport, then immediately jumps to presenting a funeral cover plan. The client says "let me think about it." What S.T.A.R. step did the advisor skip?
Spot on! Skipping discovery is the #1 reason for "let me think about it." The client wasn't ready because they hadn't been heard yet. The solution didn't feel personalised because it wasn't — it was generic.

Rejection & The Law of Averages

9 min read · Every "no" is data, not defeat

100 XP Done

Every great advisor in history has been rejected more times than accepted. The difference between those who quit and those who thrive isn't talent, skill, or luck — it's the story they tell themselves about what "no" means.

The Emotional Lie Most Agents Believe

"If I were good, I should close most people." This is completely false. Even top-performing advisors close a percentage — not everyone. Your conversion rate is your benchmark, not your failure.

The Reality of 100 Conversations

😐
60 people

Won't be interested — wrong timing, wrong mindset, wrong day. Not your fault.

🤔
25 people

Will think about it — they're interested but need more information or time.

💬
15 people

Will genuinely engage — they have a need and are open to a solution.

✅
5–10 people

Will buy. That's not failure — that's normal distribution.

The Taxi Rank Mentality

A taxi driver doesn't cry because one passenger said no — it keeps moving because there are always more people. Sales is the same. Next call = next opportunity. The rejection didn't cost you anything. Stopping costs you everything.

"You are not being rejected — you are collecting data toward your next yes."

Objection Handling — The Professional Response

8 min read · An objection is not a no — it's a question in disguise

100 XP Done

An objection is the client's way of saying: "I'm not fully convinced yet — help me get there." Advisors who treat objections as the end of a conversation never develop. Advisors who treat objections as invitations to continue become unstoppable.

The F.F.F. Technique

Feel — "I completely understand how you feel..."
Felt — "Many of my clients felt exactly the same way at first..."
Found — "But what they found was..." [bridge to solution]

This validates the concern, normalises it, and opens the door without arguing.

The 5 Most Common Objections — Click to Expand

First, clarify: "Is it the monthly budget or the timing that's the concern?" Often it's timing, not budget. If it's budget, ask what they can comfortably set aside. Then reframe the cost: "Which costs more — R200/month now, or leaving your family with nothing?"

"I understand — many people feel that way initially. What my clients found is that once they saw the real risk of not being covered, the R200 felt like the cheaper option."

This usually means: "You haven't given me enough reason to decide now." Ask: "Of course — what specific part would you like to think through? I can help you with that right now." If they can't name a specific concern, gently ask: "Is there anything that's making you hesitate?"

"Absolutely — what part would be helpful to think through together? I'd rather make sure you have full clarity than leave you with open questions."

This is valid — respect it. But don't just say "okay, call me back." Instead: "That's great — this is exactly a family decision. Can I send you a summary to share with them? And when would be a good time to get you both on a call together?"

"I love that you make financial decisions together — that's how it should be. When can the three of us connect so I can answer both your questions at once?"

Don't get defensive. Get curious: "That's great — can I ask what cover you have and how much it pays out? Many people find gaps they weren't aware of." Often they don't actually know what they have — and when they find out, there's room for you.

"Perfect — I'd love to look at what you have. Most people are surprised to find gaps. Even if everything is great, at least you'll have peace of mind knowing."

This is a reflex, not a decision. The client hasn't heard your value yet. Respond with: "I completely understand — most people say that before they realise this isn't a sales call. I'm not here to sell you anything — I just want to ask one question about your family's financial safety."

"No problem at all — I just have one 30-second question, and if the answer is no, I'll never call again. Fair?"

06

Closing with Confidence

7 min read · The natural conclusion of a great conversation

100 XP Done

The close is not a manipulative trick or a pressure tactic. It is the natural next step when a conversation has been done well. If you've built rapport, understood the client, presented the right solution, and handled objections — the close almost closes itself.

The Closing Truth

The reason most advisors struggle to close is that they start closing before the client is ready. You can't rush trust. But when trust is there, asking for the next step feels natural — not pushy.

4 Natural Closing Techniques

1
The Summary Close

"So based on everything you told me — you want R500k cover to protect your family at R180/month. That sounds right, doesn't it?" — Mirror their own words back. If they said yes throughout, they'll say yes again.

2
The Assumptive Close

"Let me get your details so we can start your cover from the 1st." — Used when the client's body language/tone shows readiness. Natural, confident, never forceful.

3
The Alternative Close

"Would you prefer to start with the essential plan at R180, or the comprehensive package at R280?" — Removes the yes/no choice. Both options move the conversation forward.

4
The Next Step Close

"I'll send you a detailed quote. Can I call you Friday at 10 to walk through it together?" — Perfect when the client needs more time. Gets a commitment to continue, not disappear.

What NOT to Do

❌ Don't beg: "Please, just think about it — I need this sale."
❌ Don't threaten: "This price won't be available after today."
❌ Don't disappear: Always confirm a next step. Never end on "let me know."
❌ Don't rush: Closing too early signals you care about commission, not them.

"The best close is a confident next step — not a desperate sale."
Final Scenario — Practice Close Activity
After a great conversation, a client says: "I like it — but I still want to think about it a bit more." Which is the best response?
Outstanding! You asked what they need to think through (addressing the real concern), avoided pressure tactics, and secured a next step. That's closing with confidence — not desperation.
