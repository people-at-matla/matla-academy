The FAIS Framework — Why It Exists

10 min read · The foundation every financial advisor must own

100 XP Done

Before you can render financial services legally, you need to understand the law that governs every conversation you have with a client. The Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act (FAIS Act, No. 37 of 2002) is not just legislation — it is the rulebook that defines your profession, your rights, and your obligations.

The Big Picture

FAIS was enacted to protect clients from unqualified, dishonest, or negligent financial advisors. If you understand why it exists, you will understand every obligation it places on you.

Key Definitions Under FAIS

The FAIS Act uses specific defined terms. Getting these wrong on an exam — or worse, in practice — has consequences.

Financial Product
Includes life policies, short-term insurance, friendly society benefits, shares, debentures, collective investment schemes, retirement annuities, and long-term deposits — among others. Knowing what counts as a financial product is critical: rendering services in relation to a financial product triggers FAIS obligations.
Advice
Any recommendation, guidance, or proposal of a financial nature furnished by a person to a client — in respect of the purchase, maintenance, or amendment of a financial product — for consideration or as part of a financial planning service. Note: general information about a product that is not personalised does not constitute advice.
Intermediary Service
Any act performed by a person (other than the product supplier) to assist a client in entering into or maintaining a financial product — including the submission of claims, collection of premiums, or arranging cover. This is the transactional side, as opposed to the advisory side.
Financial Services Provider (FSP)
Any person who, as a regular feature of that person's business, furnishes advice or renders intermediary services. An FSP must be licensed by the FSCA (Financial Sector Conduct Authority) — formerly the FSB (Financial Services Board).

The Role Players

🏛️
FSCA

Financial Sector Conduct Authority. The regulator that issues FSP licenses, maintains the register of providers, and enforces FAIS. Replaced the FSB in 2018.

🏢
FSP (Matla Life)

The licensed entity. Matla Life is the FSP. It holds the license and is responsible for all financial services rendered by its representatives.

🔑
Key Individual

Responsible for managing and overseeing the rendering of financial services within the FSP. Must be approved by the FSCA.

👤
Representative

You. A natural person who renders financial services under mandate of an FSP. Listed on the FSP's register and must be fit and proper.

Exam Tip

The FAIS Act was signed in 2002 and came into full effect in 2004. The FSCA replaced the FSB in April 2018. In exam questions, both names may appear — they refer to the same regulatory authority at different points in time.

AdviceIntermediary Service
"I recommend you invest in Policy X based on your needs."Submitting a client's application form to the insurer
Conducting a financial needs analysisCollecting monthly premiums on behalf of the FSP
Recommending a specific product for a specific clientProcessing a policy claim on a client's behalf
Personalised, evaluative, specific to one clientTransactional, operational, product-neutral
Without a licence, you have no right to advise. Without understanding FAIS, you cannot comply. Without compliance, your clients are at risk — and so are you.
Activity — What Counts as Advice? Knowledge Check

Understanding this distinction is a favourite exam topic. Apply it to this scenario:

Scenario: Nomsa is an advisor at Matla Life. A walk-in client asks: "Which of these two life policies should I take out?" Nomsa reviews the client's income, dependants, and existing cover, then says: "Based on your situation, I recommend Policy A — it offers better critical illness cover for your specific needs." Has Nomsa rendered advice under FAIS?
Correct. Nomsa performed a needs analysis and made a personalised recommendation — that is the definition of advice under FAIS. This means she must be authorised to provide advice in that product category, and all FAIS obligations (disclosure, record-keeping, etc.) apply.

Licensing & Fit and Proper Requirements

10 min read · Your right to advise starts here

100 XP Done

No person may render financial services — as an FSP or as a representative — unless they meet the fit and proper requirements set out in the FAIS Act and its subordinate legislation. These requirements exist to ensure that only honest, competent, and financially sound individuals advise the public.

The Four Pillars of Fit and Proper

Every representative and key individual must meet all four requirements. Failure on any one is grounds for not being authorised — or for debarment.

1
Honesty & Integrity

The person must not have a criminal record for dishonesty-related offences (theft, fraud, forgery, perjury, etc.). They must not have been found guilty of an offence in a professional capacity. No false declarations. This pillar goes to character — it cannot be trained into someone who lacks it.

2
Competence

The person must hold the minimum qualification and experience required for the category of financial product they render services in. They must also complete Continuous Professional Development (CPD). Competence is product-category specific — a person may be competent in Long-Term Insurance but not in Investments.

3
Operational Ability

The person must have the ability to manage and oversee the business of the FSP (for key individuals) or render the specific service (for representatives). This includes having adequate systems, processes, and infrastructure to meet regulatory obligations.

4
Financial Soundness

The person must not be under curatorship, administration, or subject to a sequestration order. A person who is over-indebted is more susceptible to financial crime. The FSCA expects its licensees to be financially responsible.

FSP Licence Categories

The FSCA issues licences for specific categories of financial products. An FSP (and its representatives) may only render services in the categories for which they are licensed.

📋
Category I

Standard FSP — may render advice and intermediary services. Must meet all fit and proper requirements.

📊
Category II

Discretionary FSP — may make investment decisions on behalf of clients without prior consent for each transaction.

🏦
Category IIA

Hedge fund FSP — may manage hedge fund portfolios. Subject to heightened requirements.

🌐
Category III

Administrative FSP — provides administrative services to clients (e.g. fund administration) without rendering advice.

💼
Category IV

Assistance business — provides assistance services such as motor claims assistance, legal advice services, etc.

Critical Point

Matla Life is a Category I FSP licensed to provide advice and intermediary services in Long-Term Insurance subcategory B1 (life policies including funeral products). As a representative, you may only advise on products within this category unless additional authorisation is obtained.

Representatives & Key Individuals

10 min read · Know your role, your responsibilities, and the consequences of getting it wrong

100 XP Done

Within the FAIS ecosystem, every person has a defined role. Understanding the distinction between a Representative and a Key Individual — and what happens when either fails to meet their obligations — is essential knowledge for the RE5 (and RE1 for Key Individuals).

RepresentativeKey Individual (KI)
Natural personNatural person
Renders financial services under mandate of FSPManages and oversees the rendering of services within the FSP
Listed on the FSP's register of representativesApproved by the FSCA before assuming the role
Must meet fit and proper requirementsMust meet higher fit and proper requirements (more experience, higher qualifications)
May or may not be an employee (can be an independent contractor)Can be a director, member, employee, or trustee of the FSP

Debarment — The Most Serious Consequence

Debarment is the removal of a representative's (or key individual's) authority to render financial services. It is not a punishment — it is a protective measure. When a representative is no longer fit and proper, the FSP has a legal obligation to debar them.

When Debarment is Mandatory

An FSP must debar a representative if the representative: no longer meets the fit and proper requirements; has been found guilty of a dishonesty-related offence; misrepresented information to clients; acted without authority; or materially failed to comply with the FAIS Act or the General Code of Conduct.

1
FSP Notifies the Representative

The FSP must give the representative notice and an opportunity to make representations before debarment — unless an urgent debarment is necessary to prevent further harm.

2
FSCA is Notified Within 15 Days

The FSP must notify the FSCA of the debarment within 15 days. The representative's name is removed from the FSP register and added to the FSCA's debarment register.

3
Representative Cannot Work Until Reinstated

A debarred person may not render financial services. They cannot simply join another FSP and start working. Reinstatement requires that the person address the grounds for debarment and apply to the FSCA.

Common Exam Trap

Many candidates confuse debarment (by the FSP) with withdrawal of licence (by the FSCA). Debarment removes a person's ability to represent an FSP. Withdrawal of licence removes the FSP's right to operate. Both are serious — but they are legally distinct actions taken by different parties.

The Register of Representatives

Every FSP must maintain a register of all its representatives. This register must include: the representative's full name, date of appointment, product categories they are authorised for, and supervision status. The FSCA may inspect this register at any time.

Your authority to advise is not a right — it is a privilege that is earned, maintained, and can be taken away. Treat it accordingly.
Activity — The Debarment Decision Scenario

Test your understanding of the FSP's legal obligations:

Scenario: Lerato, a representative at Matla Life, is found to have provided false information to three clients — leading them to buy policies they didn't need. Her manager, Sipho, discovers this. Matla's compliance officer confirms the findings. What must Matla Life do?
Correct. This is a mandatory debarment. Providing false information to clients is a failure of the honesty and integrity requirement — one of the four pillars of fit and proper. Matla Life has no discretion here. Allowing a debarrable representative to remain in service exposes the FSP to regulatory action by the FSCA.

Supervision Arrangements & CPD

10 min read · How new advisors are managed, and how all advisors stay competent

100 XP Done

Competence under FAIS is not a once-off event — it is an ongoing obligation. Two mechanisms protect clients from under-qualified advisors: Supervision Arrangements for new entrants, and Continuous Professional Development (CPD) for all authorised advisors.

Supervision Arrangements

When Supervision Is Required

A representative must be placed under supervision when they do not yet meet all the competency requirements for the product category in which they render services. This typically applies to new entrants, but also to experienced advisors moving into a new product subcategory.

📄
Written Supervision Plan

Must be documented. It outlines the competency gaps, the plan to address them, and the expected timeline for becoming fully authorised.

👨‍🏫
Competent Supervisor

The supervisor must themselves be fully authorised in the relevant product category. They cannot be under supervision themselves.

📅
Regular Assessment

The supervisor must regularly assess the representative's competency progress. Supervision is active — not just a rubber stamp.

⏱️
Defined Period

New entrants must meet all competency requirements within a defined period (typically 12–48 months depending on qualifications). Failure to do so means debarment.

Continuous Professional Development (CPD)

CPD is the ongoing training requirement that ensures all authorised representatives and key individuals stay current with industry developments, regulatory changes, and product updates.

CPD Cycle
CPD is measured in annual cycles. All authorised representatives must complete a prescribed number of CPD hours per cycle. Hours are split between structured activities (formal training, workshops, seminars, accredited courses) and unstructured activities (self-study, reading, online learning). Records must be kept for a minimum of 5 years.
Record-Keeping Obligation

You must keep evidence of all CPD activities — certificates, attendance registers, proof of completion — for 5 years. The FSCA may request these records during a compliance inspection. Failure to produce them suggests the CPD was not completed.

Structured CPDUnstructured CPD
Formal workshops and seminarsSelf-directed reading of industry publications
Accredited training programmesOnline articles and regulatory updates
Industry conferencesOn-the-job learning (limited recognition)
Provider-led product trainingPeer discussion and study groups
A supervisor who is absent, rubber-stamping, or unqualified is not protecting the client — they are creating a liability. Supervision is a responsibility, not a title.
Activity — Supervision Red Flag Scenario

Not all supervision arrangements are compliant. Identify the problem:

Scenario: Thabo joined Matla Life three months ago and is working under a supervision arrangement. His supervisor, Zanele, manages a large book of business and is frequently out of the office. On most days, Thabo signs off his own client applications and submits them directly. Zanele reviews his files at the end of each month. Is this supervision arrangement compliant?
Correct. Supervision must be active and real. A supervisor who reviews files retroactively — rather than providing contemporaneous oversight — is not fulfilling the legal obligation. Matla Life is exposed here: if Thabo gives bad advice during this period, the FSP cannot claim that proper supervision was in place.

The General Code of Conduct

10 min read · Your obligations to clients in every interaction

100 XP Done

The General Code of Conduct for Authorised Financial Services Providers and Representatives (Board Notice 80 of 2003, as amended) is the practical rulebook that governs your day-to-day client interactions. If FAIS is the law, the Code is how you live it.

The Overriding Principle (Section 2)

All FSPs and representatives must at all times render financial services honestly, fairly, with due skill, care and diligence, and in the interests of clients. This principle overrides all other specifics in the Code. When in doubt, ask: am I acting in my client's interest?

Disclosure Obligations (Section 4)

Before rendering any financial service, you must disclose the following to the client — in plain language:

A
Identity of the FSP

The name of the FSP (Matla Life), its FSCA licence number, and its physical address. The client must know who is responsible for the advice they receive.

B
Status as an Intermediary or Advisor

Whether you are acting as an advisor, as an intermediary, or as both — and whether you are acting independently or under mandate of a specific FSP.

C
Remuneration & Conflict of Interest

Whether the FSP or representative receives commission, fees, or other benefits from the product supplier. Clients have a right to know if your recommendation may be influenced by financial incentives.

The Needs Analysis (Section 8)

Before giving advice, you must conduct a thorough needs analysis. This means understanding the client's:

💰
Financial Position

Income, expenses, existing policies, debts, savings. Can they afford the premium? What are they already covered for?

🎯
Financial Needs

What specific risk or financial goal is the client trying to address? Funeral cover? Life cover? Retirement?

⚠️
Risk Tolerance

For investment products — what level of risk is the client comfortable with? This determines appropriate product selection.

📋
Existing Products

Are there existing policies that should be considered? Replacement of an existing product triggers additional obligations.

Record-Keeping

5-Year Record Retention
All records of client interactions, advice given, disclosures made, and product information provided must be kept for a minimum of 5 years. This includes needs analysis documents, signed disclosure forms, policy documents, and correspondence. The client also has the right to request a copy of any record relating to them.
Replacement Products — Extra Caution

If you recommend that a client replace an existing financial product with a new one, you must: disclose all material differences between the old and new product; explain all costs and consequences of replacement; get the client's signed consent; and submit the replacement notification to the FSCA within the prescribed period. Unsolicited replacement recommendations expose you to serious regulatory risk.

Activity — Disclosure Compliance Knowledge Check

A disclosure violation is one of the most common FAIS complaints. Can you spot it?

Scenario: Zanele is passionate about life insurance and, when she meets a new client, she gets straight into explaining the Matla funeral policy — how it works, its benefits, and the premiums. The client asks questions and Zanele answers them thoroughly. After 20 minutes, the client says she's interested. At no point did Zanele introduce herself as a Matla Life representative, mention the FSP's license number, or disclose her commission structure. Has Zanele complied with the General Code of Conduct?
Correct. Section 4 of the General Code of Conduct requires disclosure before rendering any financial service. This means the FSP name, licence number, and disclosure of remuneration must be given to the client at the very start — not at the end, not only at sign-up. Zanele is in breach, even though her product explanation was thorough.

FICA & Money Laundering

10 min read · The obligation to know your client — and protect the financial system

100 XP Done

The Financial Intelligence Centre Act (FICA, No. 38 of 2001) is South Africa's primary anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing legislation. As a financial services provider, Matla Life is an Accountable Institution — meaning its representatives have specific legal obligations under FICA that exist alongside their FAIS obligations.

The Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC)

The FIC is the South African government's financial intelligence unit. It collects, analyses, and distributes financial intelligence. It is not an investigative body — it does not arrest people. It passes intelligence to SAPS, SARS, the NPA, and other enforcement agencies.

The Three Stages of Money Laundering

1
Placement — Getting Dirty Money Into the System

The criminal introduces illegal funds into the legitimate financial system. Classic examples: cash deposits into bank accounts, purchasing insurance with cash, currency exchange. This is when the money is most visible and most detectable — and why cash transaction reporting is critical.

2
Layering — Disguising the Trail

The criminal moves the money through multiple transactions to create distance between it and its source. Examples: transferring between accounts, converting to foreign currency, purchasing and selling assets, policy surrenders. The goal is to make it impossible to trace the money back to the crime.

3
Integration — Making It Look Legitimate

The money is now "clean" and is re-introduced into the legitimate economy. The criminal can now spend it without arousing suspicion — buying property, making business investments, or living a lavish lifestyle with apparently legitimate income.

Your FICA Obligations as an Accountable Institution

🆔
Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

Verify the identity of every client before entering into a business relationship. Obtain and verify ID documents, proof of address, and the purpose of the relationship.

🔍
Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

Apply EDD to high-risk clients: Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), clients from high-risk jurisdictions, or those with complex ownership structures. More scrutiny, more verification.

🚨
Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs)

If you suspect a transaction involves proceeds of crime or terrorist financing — report it to the FIC immediately. There is no minimum threshold. You must not tip off the client that you've reported them.

💵
Cash Threshold Reports (CTRs)

Any cash transaction of R24,999.99 or more must be reported to the FIC — regardless of whether you suspect anything. This is an automatic obligation.

Tipping Off — A Criminal Offence

If you have filed or are about to file an STR, you are prohibited from telling the client — or anyone associated with them — that you have done so. "Tipping off" is a criminal offence under FICA. The integrity of the FIC's intelligence depends on the element of surprise.

Activity — FICA in Practice Scenario

FICA obligations require you to act — not look the other way. Test your instinct:

Scenario: Mr Khumalo visits your office and asks to pay six months of life insurance premiums upfront — in cash. The total is R36,000. He seems nervous and cannot clearly explain where the money came from, saying only "I've been saving." He wants to pay quickly and leave. What do you do?
Correct. Two separate obligations apply here. The CTR is automatic — any cash of R24,999+ must be reported to the FIC regardless of suspicion. The STR applies because of the nervous behaviour and inability to explain the source of funds. Critically: you must NOT tell Mr Khumalo you have reported him — tipping off is a criminal offence under FICA.

Complaints Handling & the FAIS Ombud

10 min read · When things go wrong — and your legal obligations when they do

100 XP Done

Even the best advisors receive complaints. FAIS does not expect perfection — it expects a proper process. Understanding how to handle complaints professionally protects your clients, your FSP, and your career.

Two-Tier System

South Africa's FAIS complaints system is two-tiered. The client must first go through the FSP's internal complaints resolution process. Only if that process fails — or takes longer than 6 weeks — can the client escalate to the FAIS Ombud.

Internal Complaints — What Your FSP Must Have

1
A Written Complaints Policy

Every FSP must have a documented complaints resolution procedure. Clients must be informed of this procedure — in writing — before or at the time of entering a business relationship.

2
Acknowledge Within a Reasonable Time

The FSP must acknowledge receipt of the complaint promptly and keep the client updated on progress. Silence is not acceptable — and can itself be grounds for escalation to the Ombud.

3
Resolve or Respond Within 6 Weeks

If the FSP cannot resolve the complaint within 6 weeks, the client must be informed — in writing — of the right to take the matter to the FAIS Ombud. The FSP cannot prevent this escalation.

The FAIS Ombud

FAIS Ombud — Office of the Ombud for Financial Services Providers
An independent statutory body established under the FAIS Act. The Ombud resolves complaints between clients and FSPs through a process of investigation and determination — free of charge to the complainant. Determinations are legally binding. The Ombud may award compensation and, for serious cases, refer matters to the FSCA for further action.
⚖️
Jurisdiction

The Ombud can hear complaints about advice given, intermediary services rendered, or the conduct of an FSP or representative — provided the complaint falls within the FAIS Act's scope.

📅
Prescription

A complaint must be lodged within 3 years of the date the complainant became aware of the act or omission that caused the loss.

💰
Maximum Award

The Ombud may award compensation to the complainant. The maximum award is updated periodically — check the Ombud's website for the current limit.

🆓
Free to Complainant

The FAIS Ombud's service is completely free to clients. This is by design — the process must not be a barrier to access for ordinary South Africans.

Penalties for Non-Compliance Under FAIS

The Consequences Are Real

FAIS non-compliance can result in: administrative penalties (fines); suspension or withdrawal of the FSP's licence; debarment of individuals; and in the most serious cases, criminal prosecution. A conviction under FAIS can permanently end a career in financial services.

The FAIS Ombud handles individual complaints between clients and FSPs — it is a dispute resolution body. The FSCA is the regulatory authority — it licenses FSPs, sets standards, and can impose sanctions. They have different functions but work in a complementary way. The complaint is not the crisis — how you handle it is. A well-managed complaint can restore trust. A badly handled one can end careers and licences.
